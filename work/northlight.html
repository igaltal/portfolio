Case study · 2024 · Design systems

Northlight: a design system that compiles.

A growing fintech company had four products, three frontend stacks, and no shared visual language. I designed and built Northlight: a token-first system where Figma and code read from the same source of truth. Consistency stopped depending on discipline.

01 · Overview

One language for four products.

Northlight is the design system I built for a Series-C fintech with a consumer app, a merchant dashboard, an internal ops console, and a public site. Its users are the makers: 6 product designers and about 30 frontend engineers, who were each solving the same button, table, and form questions again every week.

The problem it solves: inconsistency was a tax on everything. Slower builds, visual drift that hurt customer trust in a product that holds people's money, and accessibility problems found only in audits. Northlight makes the consistent way the easy way.

02 · Problem

Fourteen greys and no answers.

User pain (designers & engineers)

The audit found 14 shades of grey, 9 button styles, and 5 modal patterns in production. Designers spent hours redrawing basic parts. Engineers guessed spacing from screenshots. Reviews became arguments about taste instead of logic. New hires needed weeks to learn "how we do things here", because that knowledge lived only in people's heads.

Business pain

Every feature estimate carried a hidden UI cost: about a third of frontend time went to rebuilding existing patterns. A brand refresh was priced at a full quarter of engineering time, because color values were hard-coded in about 3,800 places. And in fintech, visual drift is not only a style problem: an inconsistent product looks less trustworthy exactly where trust matters most.

What was tried before, and why it failed

  • A shared Figma library (the previous attempt). It drifted away from the code within weeks. Nobody trusted it, so nobody maintained it.
  • Using a ready-made system (MUI and others). It fixed consistency by erasing the brand, and its theming could not handle the product's dense financial tables.
  • "Just be more careful." Discipline does not scale past one team. Shipping always wins.

04 · Research

Audit the product, interview the makers.

A full UI inventory across 4 products (612 screens), interviews with all 6 designers and 12 engineers, and a close look at Polaris, Carbon, Primer, and Base, to borrow structure, not style.

Drift starts at handoff, not at design

The designers were more consistent than the shipped product. The change happened in translation: engineers estimated values that were not exactly written down, or were written in units the code does not use.

Engineers wanted an API, not a style guide

Nobody reads PDF guides. Engineers asked for autocomplete: "if I type Button variant= and the options show up, I'll use them." Adoption is a comfort problem.

The densest screens got the least help

Ready-made systems are built for marketing pages and settings forms. This product lives in transaction tables, ledgers, and reconciliation views. Density had to be a first-class option, not a hack.

Governance decides if a system lives or dies

The previous library died because nobody owned the backlog. Contributors need a path: propose, review, ship, with a named owner and a clear response time. Process design is product design.

08 · Contribution flow

Governance drawn as a flow, because it is one.

The system's most important "user flow" is not on a screen. It is how a missing component becomes a shipped one, without the system team becoming a bottleneck.

Key decision: a "declined" answer still unblocks the maker. They ship a local one-off with a marker that lint can track. If three products grow the same one-off, that is automatic proof it belongs in the system. The system grows from real demand, never from guesses.

09 · Wireframes

Wireframing a component, not a page.

For a system, "wireframes" means structure studies. The data table, the product's hardest-working component, went through three rounds.

Round 1: one big component

One <Table> with 30 props. Covered every case, readable in none. Dropped: too many props is just drift in a uniform.

Round 2: slot regions

Toolbar, header, row, footer as slots. Flexible, but designers could not tell which combinations were allowed.

Round 3: primitives + named recipes

Slots stay for flexibility. Named recipes ("TransactionsTable", "AuditLog") define the allowed combinations. Designers pick recipes, engineers get both. Shipped.

10 · Design exploration

Choosing the system's temperature.

Three visual languages, each applied to the same three screens (ledger, onboarding, empty state), and reviewed with brand, product, and compliance.

A: "Institutional"

Navy, serif headings, very formal. It read as trustworthy, and also as a bank from 1998. It failed the consumer app's need for warmth.

Rejected. Trust without friendliness.

B: "Neo-brut fintech"

Strong colors, thick borders, playful. It gave the consumer app energy, but hurt the ops console, where operators move real money and need calm.

Rejected. Could not cover both light and dense screens.
Shipped

C: "Quiet precision"

Neutral surfaces, one clear indigo, and status colors used only for money states. Warmth comes from type and motion, not decoration. Works from a marketing page to a reconciliation table.

Selected. One language, four products, no translation.

Component contract

Primary Secondary Tertiary Disabled

Every component ships with four things: variants matched 1:1 between Figma and React (same prop names), auto-layout that matches the flex behavior, keyboard and screen-reader support as acceptance criteria, and a visual regression test. A component is not "done" until all four exist. That definition of done is the design system.

13 · Living documentation

Docs that can't lie.

Northlight's documentation site renders the real components from the real package. Every example is live code, so the docs cannot drift away from production. Each page shows the rendered component next to its Figma version, its token dependencies, and do/don't examples. Interactive prop playgrounds replaced the static spec sheets that nobody read.

The fine detail lives in motion: hover, focus, pressed, loading, and error states are shown as small interactions, with their motion tokens written next to them. motion/decisive 240ms for actions, motion/ambient 480ms for things that arrive. The same grammar this portfolio uses.

14 · Challenges

The hard decisions.

14.1Migrate everything at once, or step by step?

Considered: one big migration
A clean break and a great demo. Also a full quarter of feature freeze across four products, a cost the business had already refused once, and rightly so.
Shipped: step-by-step adoption
New screens must use Northlight. Old screens migrate when someone touches them anyway. A dashboard tracked component coverage per product, published monthly. Coverage passed 80% in month 8, with zero feature freezes.

14.2Who is allowed to say no?

Considered: the system team as gatekeeper
It guarantees consistency, and it guarantees anger. Every central UI team before this one had become a queue that people worked around.
Shipped: shared ownership, central standards
Any team can build a component. The system team owns the standards (tokens only, accessibility, tests) and the 48-hour triage. Quality is enforced by CI, not by meetings.

14.3Technical limit: three frontend stacks

Considered: components for every stack
React, Vue, and an old server-rendered app. That means triple maintenance forever, and the system team was 2 people.
Shipped: tokens everywhere, components where they pay off
Tokens compile to plain CSS variables that any stack can use. React components for the two products in active development. The legacy app got token-based CSS and a sunset date. Honest scope beats heroic scope.

Lessons learned

  • Adoption is the product. Comfort (autocomplete, defaults, lint) moved coverage more than any presentation did.
  • Governance is a designed flow. The 48-hour triage promise did more for the system's reputation than any single component.
  • Name the recipes. Primitives give power. Names give alignment. The breakthrough was shipping both.

Future improvements

  • Density as a user preference, not only a product decision.
  • Motion tokens in the native mobile apps.
  • An AI review bot that flags raw values in PRs and suggests the nearest token.
