Case study · 2024 · Product & UX research

Cadence: a calendar that protects your focus.

Every calendar works for the people who want your time. Cadence flips the default: deep work is scheduled first, and meetings negotiate for what is left. A research-heavy project where the breakthrough was a scheduling model, not a screen.

01 · Overview

The calendar works against its owner.

Cadence is a scheduling product for knowledge workers whose output depends on uninterrupted focus: engineers, designers, writers, analysts. It syncs with Google and Outlook calendars, learns when a person does their best work, and reserves that time before the meeting requests arrive.

The problem it solves: normal calendars treat empty space as free space. For makers, the empty space is the work. Cadence makes focus time a real calendar object, with the same social weight as a meeting.

02 · Problem

Fifteen reasonable meetings can kill a week.

User pain

No single meeting destroys a week. The pattern does. Participants described days cut into 40-minute pieces, each piece too short to start anything real. The feeling they described was not stress. It was sadness about the work that never happened, plus guilt when they tried to protect time ("blocking my calendar feels like lying").

Business pain

For employers, broken maker time is invisible cost: senior engineers doing their real work at night, people leaving with "no time to think" in the exit interview, projects slipping with no single cause to fix. For us, the market signal was clear. The big calendar tools all have "focus time" features, and almost nobody uses them. The problem is real and still open.

Why existing solutions are not enough

  • Manual calendar blocks. Socially weak ("it's just a block, I'll book over it") and always the first thing to be given up.
  • Built-in focus-time features. Add-ons that schedule leftovers. They fill the gaps after the meetings land, which is exactly backwards.
  • Scheduling-link tools. They optimize for the person booking the time, which makes the problem worse for the person being booked.

04 · Research

Fourteen interviews, 40 calendar audits, one reframe.

Interviews with 14 makers and 4 managers. Opt-in audits of 40 real calendars (8 weeks of history). A diary study through one full sprint. A close look at Clockwise, Reclaim, Motion, and the built-in focus features.

The enemy is fragmentation, not volume

The audited calendars averaged only 3.1 meeting hours per day. But the meetings were placed so that the longest free stretch averaged 51 minutes. People don't need fewer meetings. They need meetings grouped together.

Protecting time is a social act, not a feature

Blocks failed because they had no social weight: a block that just says "Busy" reads as optional. Protection needs to be visible, explained, and understood by the whole team to survive.

Energy has a shape

Every participant knew their best hours ("I'm useless after 3pm") and no tool ever asked. Putting focus time into low-energy leftover slots is why the built-in features felt like spam.

People must stay in control

Tools that moved events by themselves were dropped within weeks: "it rearranged my day and I stopped trusting the calendar at all." Suggest, never force.

"I can survive any Tuesday. What I can't survive is every day being Tuesday." P4, staff engineer, calendar audit debrief

08 · User flow

The defend loop.

The product's defining flow is not creating a focus block. It is what happens when a meeting request lands on top of one.

The key decision: Cadence negotiates for the user, but never behind their back. Auto-negotiation sends the requester two concrete alternative slots. That is a helpful counter-offer, not a rejection. Research insight 04 (control) shaped every arrow in this diagram.

09 · Wireframes

Finding the shape of the week.

Round 1: familiar grid

A standard week view with focus blocks as events. Testers could not see the fragmentation. Everything looked equally fine. The layout hid the exact problem the product solves.

Round 2: fragmentation bars

Each day as one bar: solid means unbroken stretches, hollow means pieces. The "oh no" moment landed instantly, but people could not act on it. Insight without control.

Round 3: grid + report strip

The familiar grid stays (control), with the fragmentation strip on top (insight). Tapping the strip highlights grouping moves on the grid below. Shipped.

10 · Design exploration

How pushy should software be about your time?

The exploration axis was not visual. It was personality. The same screens, three voices, each tested as a working prototype for one week.

A: "The Coach"

Cheerful, streaks, celebration copy ("3 deep days in a row! 🔥"). Testers liked it in week one and felt talked-down-to by week two. Turning focus into a game made it feel cheap.

Rejected. Motivation is not decoration.

B: "The Chief of Staff"

Fully automatic: rearranges your week overnight for the best focus. The calendars were objectively better, and people hated it. It confirmed the control finding: trust died on the first surprise.

Rejected. Optimal but untrusted is worthless.
Shipped

C: "The Diplomat"

Calm, factual, suggest-only. States the cost ("this invite splits your longest stretch"), offers moves, acts only after approval. No exclamation marks, no guilt.

Selected. Clear about facts, careful about actions.

DESKTOP Planning surface

Week grid + fragmentation strip + drag-to-group. The Sunday planning ritual takes four minutes.

MOBILE Decision surface

Today view and defend prompts only. On purpose, it cannot do planning. Phones are where plans get defended, not made.

CALENDAR NATIVE Everyone else's surface

Focus blocks show up in Google and Outlook with clear names and the negotiation link. The product works on colleagues who never installed it.

12 · Design system

A palette with exactly one loud color.

Color · time semantics

time/focusthe one strong color
time/meetingneutral, meetings are not the enemy
time/tentativestriped, not settled
signal/conflictamber, never red

Focus is the only saturated color on the canvas. The visual hierarchy is the value proposition. Conflict is amber, because a meeting request is a negotiation, not an emergency.

Motion & components

Protect this time Suggest new slot See the cost

22 components on a 4px grid, tokens shared between web and mobile. The motion rule: time never teleports. When a block moves, it slides through the hours it crosses (280ms), so cause and effect stay clear. Reduced-motion swaps slides for fades, and never removes the state change.

13 · Prototype

Tested on real calendars, or not at all.

A static prototype of a calendar is fiction. The product only shows its value against the mess of a real week. So I built the prototype as a working web app with read-only Google Calendar sync, from day three. Eight participants ran their real weeks through it during the diary study.

The signature micro-interaction came from this phase: the grouping gesture. Dragging a meeting near a cluster snaps it into place (140ms, decisive ease), and the fragmentation strip above heals in real time. The reward is watching your longest stretch grow. Testers called it "weirdly satisfying", which was exactly the goal: make the healthy action the enjoyable one.

14 · Challenges

The hard decisions.

14.1How much protection is too much?

Considered: hard blocks that cannot be booked over
Maximum protection, and users loved the idea in interviews. In practice it created social incidents. A VP bounced off a junior engineer's wall, and the story traveled. Protection that embarrasses people gets turned off.
Shipped: three protection levels
Open / negotiable / protected, with per-person exceptions. The default answer is a counter-offer, never a wall. Blocks survived at 79%, higher than hard blocks in the pilot, because nobody turned the feature off.

14.2The cold start: learning someone's energy profile

Considered: learn it from behavior
Elegant and zero-effort, but it needed three weeks of data before the product did anything smart. The activation target was week one.
Shipped: ask three questions, then learn
Onboarding asks when you think best, when you fade, and how many meetings you can take. 40 seconds. The model starts useful and improves quietly. Boring beats clever, when clever arrives late.

14.3Technical limit: guests who don't have Cadence

Considered: negotiation inside the app only
Clean and controlled, and useless. In most conflicts, the other person had never heard of the product.
Shipped: negotiation over normal calendar invites
Counter-offers travel as standard calendar responses, plus a simple web page (no login) showing the two alternative slots. The requester clicks one. Done. Designing for the non-user became the growth loop: 31% of new signups came from receiving a negotiation.

Lessons learned

  • Design the social contract, not only the interface. The product's real surface was the message a colleague receives. That is where it won or lost.
  • Show the cost, and people choose well. "This splits your longest stretch" changed behavior more than any blocking feature.
  • A research reframe beats research findings. "Fragmentation, not volume" redirected the whole product. One sentence, five months of leverage.

Future improvements

  • Team rhythms: company-level no-meeting windows, based on shared data.
  • Task-aware blocks: pull the top task from Linear into the focus card.
  • Energy model v2: quiet learning from completed blocks, still suggest-only.
