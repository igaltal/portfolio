SwipeHire: job hunting, reduced to one gesture.

01 · Overview

Swipe right to apply. The agent does the rest.

One motion: swipe right to apply. An AI agent ranks the jobs, writes each application, and tracks every reply. You approve every send.

02 · Problem

Job hunting is high friction and low feedback.

Dozens of tabs, the same form again and again, silence at the end. The goal: compress the whole thing into a gesture, under 5 minutes a day.

  • Job boards: built for browsing, not deciding.
  • Auto-apply bots: spray in your name; trust dies.
  • Spreadsheet + willpower: works as well as your worst week.
03 · Goals

Five minutes a day, and every send approved.

04 · Research

Steal the gesture, fix the meaning.

Teardowns of LinkedIn, Indeed and Otta, plus watching real job searches.

The bottleneck is deciding, not finding

One card, one decision. A deck removes the paradox of choice.

People swipe with flicks, not drags

Decisions must trigger on distance or velocity. A fast flick counts.

Automation without honesty kills trust

Honest apply-type badges up front prevent the rage-quit later.

Irreversibility creates hesitation

Undo is what makes fast swiping emotionally safe.

05 · User personas

The active seeker and the quiet browser.

A

The active seeker

Between jobs or done with the current one. Applying is their job now.
"I applied to 40 places this month. I heard back from three."
Behaviors
  • Applies daily, tracks in a spreadsheet, loses track anyway
  • Copy-pastes the same answers into different forms
Needs
  • Volume without the paperwork
  • A pipeline view: applied, pending, match, interview, offer
  • Feedback: what happened to each application
What SwipeHire gives them
  • The deck for volume, the dashboard for the funnel
  • The agent writes each application; they just approve
Q

The quiet browser

Employed, curious, "open to offers". Five minutes a day, max.
"I'm not job hunting. But if something great shows up, I want to see it."
Behaviors
  • Checks casually, on the couch, in short sessions
  • Never fills a form for a "maybe"
Needs
  • High-signal cards: is this worth my attention?
  • Zero commitment per decision, zero paperwork
  • Block companies (like their own employer) from the deck
What SwipeHire gives them
  • Match ring + why-it-matches: 2-second decisions
  • Swipe down to block a company, forever

08 · User flow

The anatomy of one swipe.

What happens between "finger down" and "application sent". Most of the product's intelligence hides inside this one gesture.

The key decision: trigger on distance or velocity, and fade the stamp in while dragging, so you know the outcome before you let go.

10 · Design exploration

Escaping the AI-purple cliché.

A voice that says "trust + career", not "chatbot".

A: "AI purple"

Violet gradients, sparkles, glow. Instantly reads as AI, and instantly reads as every other AI app. For something touching your career, it felt more gimmick than partner.

Rejected. Category cliché, wrong emotion.

B: "Dating app warm"

Leaning into the Tinder DNA: warm reds and pinks, playful copy. Fun, but it trivialised the decisions. A job is not a crush; the swipe is borrowed, the tone should not be.

Rejected. The gesture can be playful; the stakes are not.
Shipped

C: "Trust blue, glass dark"

An azure/cobalt brand blue (trust + growth) on a layered dark UI with glassmorphism surfaces, and semantic accents mapped to actions: mint for apply, flame for skip, sky for super-like, gold for warnings.

Selected. Serious enough for careers, alive enough for swiping.

DECK Immersive by design

Full screen, tab bar hidden. Nothing competes with the decision. The peeking next card (scaled 0.94, shifted down) says "there's more" without showing it.

DESKTOP A phone in the middle

On desktop the app renders inside a centered 440px phone shell. One layout to perfect, identical muscle memory everywhere.

HONESTY CUES Badges before swipes

"1-click", "Form", "Complex" badges set expectations before the swipe. A small honesty cue that prevents the frustration that kills trust in automation.

12 · Design system

Semantic color, glass surfaces, three motions.

Built on Tailwind with custom design tokens. Every accent color is mapped to a meaning, not a mood.

The azure/cobalt brand was a deliberate move away from "AI purple": trust + growth, the career world's colors. Action colors repeat everywhere: stamps, buttons, funnel stages, charts.

Surfaces, type & motion

.glass / .glass-strong two tiers of translucency + backdrop blur

chip · btn · stat-card reusable primitives, no one-off styles

Soft radial glows on the background give depth without noise. color-scheme: dark, PWA meta, and safe-area insets make it feel like a native app.

13 · The swipe deck

Physics that make a gesture feel honest.

The deck is the product's signature interaction, and where most of the interaction-design work went. Built with Framer Motion.

  • Elastic drag, rotation mapped to the finger. A card, not a div.
  • Distance or velocity triggers the decision. Flicks count.
  • Progressive stamps show the outcome before you release.
  • The next card peeks from behind at 0.94 scale.
  • Buttons mirror every gesture and teach it.
  • Undo makes fast swiping emotionally safe.
14 · Challenges

The hard decisions.

14.1Teaching a 4-direction gesture without a tutorial

Tutorial overlay
People skip it, forget it. It admits the design failed.
The interface teaches itself
Stamps mid-drag plus labeled buttons. By card three you swipe.

14.2Automation vs. the user's name on the application

Fully automatic sending
Things go out in your name unseen. Trust dies once.
The agent writes, you approve
"You approve every send" is the trust contract of the product.

14.3What counts as a "match"?

Match on every application
Rewarding for a day, then a list of silence.
Match = a real company reply
Rarer, and real. When the app says "match", something happened.

14.4International from day one

LTR now, RTL "later"
Retrofitting RTL into a gesture UI is a rewrite, not a patch.
Logical properties from day one
RTL-ready and accessible from the first component.
