01 · Overview

Swipe right to apply. The agent does the rest.

SwipeHire is a job discovery app built around one motion: swipe right to apply, left to skip, up to super-like, down to block a company. Behind the gesture, an AI career agent ranks jobs against your profile, writes each application, and tracks every reply. You approve every send.

Who it's for: people in an active job search who are tired of tabs and forms, and people who are only "open to offers" and want five useful minutes a day instead of an evening of paperwork.

02 · Problem

Job hunting is high friction and low feedback.

User pain

A normal job search means dozens of tabs, the same form filled again and again, cover letters nobody reads, and silence at the end. There is no sense of progress and no feedback loop. People burn out on the process before they burn out on the market.

The design goal

Compress "finding and applying to a job" into a gesture. Something you can do in under 5 minutes a day, on your phone, without it feeling like paperwork.

Why existing tools don't solve it

  • Job boards and LinkedIn. Search, filter, click, form, repeat. Built for browsing, not deciding. "Easy Apply" still means a form, and quality signal is thin.
  • Auto-apply bots. They spray applications you never saw. Recruiters can tell, users lose trust, and a "match" means nothing.
  • Spreadsheet + willpower. The default system. It works exactly as well as your discipline on a bad week.

04 · Research

Steal the gesture, fix the meaning.

I tore down the flows of LinkedIn, Indeed, and Otta, studied how dating apps teach swiping, and watched friends run their job searches (mostly: many tabs, one spreadsheet, and dread).

The bottleneck is deciding, not finding

Job seekers don't lack listings. They lack a fast, low-effort way to say yes or no and move on. A deck of cards with one decision at a time removes the paradox of choice that a list creates.

People swipe with flicks, not drags

Watching real swiping: quick short flicks, not long careful drags. So decisions must trigger on distance or velocity. A fast flick counts even if it's short. Respecting this is what makes the gesture feel natural.

Automation without honesty kills trust

People had been burned by "1-click apply" that turned into a 20-minute form. Setting expectations up front (1-click / Form / Complex badges) prevents the frustration that makes users quit.

Irreversibility creates hesitation

When a swipe cannot be taken back, people slow down and the whole model collapses. Undo for the last swipe is not a nice-to-have. It's what makes fast swiping emotionally safe.

08 · User flow

The anatomy of one swipe.

What happens between "finger down" and "application sent". Most of the product's intelligence hides inside this one gesture.

The key decision: triggering on either distance or velocity. A quick flick counts even if it's short, because that is how people actually swipe. And the stamp fades in progressively with drag distance, so you always know what will happen before you let go. That is the core affordance that makes a 4-direction gesture learnable.

09 · Wireframes

How much job fits on one card?

The card had to answer "is this worth a swipe?" in about 2 seconds, while carrying a lot of data. Three structures.

Study 1: minimal card

Logo, title, company, one line. Beautiful and fast, but people could not decide: every swipe felt like a guess. Dropped: speed without information is just gambling.

Study 2: the full posting

Everything on the card, scroll to read. Confident decisions, but each one took a minute. The "5 minutes a day" promise died. Dropped.

Study 3: fixed header + depth on scroll

Header is fixed: company, title, match ring, key chips. The body scrolls for depth: why-it-matches, skills, missing skills, salary, benefits. A pinned "view full posting" link is always reachable. Shipped.

10 · Design exploration

Escaping the AI-purple cliché.

Every AI product in 2025 shipped in purple gradients. The visual exploration was about finding a voice that says "trust + career", not "chatbot".

A: "AI purple"

Violet gradients, sparkles, glow. Instantly reads as AI, and instantly reads as every other AI app. For something touching your career, it felt more gimmick than partner.

Rejected. Category cliché, wrong emotion.

B: "Dating app warm"

Leaning into the Tinder DNA: warm reds and pinks, playful copy. Fun, but it trivialised the decisions. A job is not a crush; the swipe is borrowed, the tone should not be.

Rejected. The gesture can be playful; the stakes are not.
Shipped

C: "Trust blue, glass dark"

An azure/cobalt brand blue (trust + growth) on a layered dark UI with glassmorphism surfaces, and semantic accents mapped to actions: mint for apply, flame for skip, sky for super-like, gold for warnings.

Selected. Serious enough for careers, alive enough for swiping.

13 · The swipe deck

Physics that make a gesture feel honest.

The deck is the product's signature interaction, and where most of the interaction-design work went. Built with Framer Motion.

The gesture

  • Elastic drag with mapped rotation. The card follows the finger, rotating from -18° to +18° with horizontal offset, so it feels like a physical card, not a div.
  • Distance or velocity. A decision triggers on either. A quick flick counts even if it's short, because that is how people actually swipe.
  • Spring everything. Snap-back spring if the gesture is unclear; a directional fly-out spring when committed.

The feedback

  • Progressive stamps. As you drag, a color tint, border, and action stamp (APPLY / SKIP / SUPER / BLOCK) fade in with distance. You always know what will happen before you release.
  • The peeking card. The next card sits behind at 0.94 scale, communicating depth and "there's more".

The safety net

  • Every gesture has a labeled button. Accessible, and discoverable for first-timers. The buttons teach the gestures.
  • Undo the last swipe. And an "all caught up" empty state with a reset. Fast swiping is only possible when mistakes are cheap.
14 · Challenges

The hard decisions.

14.1Teaching a 4-direction gesture without a tutorial

Considered: an onboarding tutorial overlay
The standard fix: arrows and tooltips on first launch. People skip it, forget it, and it admits the design failed to explain itself.
Shipped: the interface teaches while you use it
Progressive stamps show the outcome mid-drag, and every gesture has a labeled button doing the same thing. First-timers tap the buttons and see the deck respond; by the third card they're swiping. No tutorial screen exists.

14.2Automation vs. the user's name on the application

Considered: fully automatic sending
Swipe right, application gone. Maximum speed, and the numbers look great, until users realise things were sent in their name that they never saw. Trust dies once and never comes back.
Shipped: the agent writes, you approve
Every application is drafted by the agent and approved by the user before sending. "Free. You approve every send." is on the landing page because it is the trust contract of the whole product.

14.3What counts as a "match"?

Considered: match on every application
Like dating apps: swipe right = match. Feels rewarding for a day, then "matches" becomes a list of silence, and the word loses all meaning.
Shipped: a match forms only on a positive company reply
An intentional emotional-design choice. Matches are rarer and real. When the app says "match", something actually happened.

14.4International from day one

Considered: LTR now, RTL "later"
The classic debt. Retrofitting RTL into a gesture-driven, position-sensitive UI later is a rewrite, not a patch.
Shipped: logical properties from the ground up
dir="rtl" support, margin-inline, text-align: start throughout. Plus aria-labels on icon-only buttons, semantic headings, and a dedicated accessibility page.
15 · Results

A job search that fits in a coffee break.

SwipeHire is live, with the full loop working end to end.

What shipped

  • The swipe deck with physics-based gestures, stamps, undo, and empty states
  • The job card: match-score ring, apply-type badges, why-it-matches, salary, apply-confidence
  • The AI agent loop: ranking, application writing, approval, reply tracking
  • Matches pipeline and analytics dashboard built on one funnel model
  • AI Coach, profile, onboarding, and settings
  • A complete design system: semantic tokens, glass surfaces, three motion presets
  • RTL-ready, accessible, PWA-grade mobile experience

Lessons learned

  • Design for the gesture, not the form. Reducing a multi-step, form-heavy flow to one motion changed how the product feels more than any visual decision.
  • Layered feedback makes novel interactions learnable. Stamps + tint + buttons taught a 4-way gesture with zero tutorial.
  • Trust is a feature you ship. Approve-every-send, honest badges, meaningful matches, undo. Each one costs speed and buys the product its life.

What's next

  • Salary negotiation guidance from the Coach
  • Team/referral features: share a card with a friend
