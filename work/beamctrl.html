01 · Overview

A lighting console that lives in the browser.

BEAM-CTRL controls DMX lighting rigs (moving heads, RGBW pars, lasers, fog) in real time. The full console runs in a browser. A free desktop app (Electron) bridges to the physical USB-DMX interface. And because everything speaks the same WebSocket protocol, a phone or a tablet becomes a live remote for the same show: register, download, sign in with the same account, plug in your interface.

Who it's for: mobile DJs, small venues, and event lighting operators who need real control, but for whom professional consoles like grandMA are too expensive and too complex.

02 · Problem

Between the toy apps and the €40k console, there's nothing.

User pain

A mobile DJ with six moving heads has bad options. Professional software (grandMA, Daslight, Avolites) is powerful, desktop-bound, and takes weeks to learn. Cheap mobile apps are toys that fall apart on a real gig. And in every case, the operator is chained to the booth: to change a look mid-show, you walk back to the laptop.

The moment that matters

Live shows punish latency and mistakes instantly. If the blackout button is two menus deep, or a slider lags half a second, the product is unusable, no matter how pretty. The whole design starts from the worst moment: mid-show, dark room, sweaty hands, something needs to change now.

Why existing solutions fall short

  • Pro consoles & software. Built for lighting designers with training. Overkill in cost and complexity for a DJ or a bar.
  • Consumer lighting apps. Handle one fixture type, no real programming model, die when the rig grows.
  • Sound-active mode on the fixture. The default fallback: lights blink at the beat with zero intent. It's what people settle for, not what they want.

04 · Research

Learning from the consoles people already trust.

I studied Daslight 5, grandMA and club console conventions in depth, and watched how DJs and lighting operators actually work a show: where their hands go, what they touch every minute, and what they touch once a year.

Muscle memory is the real spec

Lighting operators think in banks, scenes, chases, and cues. Inventing new words or layouts for these would make the product harder, not simpler. That is why the Programs tab deliberately mirrors Daslight: pros bring their habits with them.

Programming and performing are different mindsets

Before the show you build carefully. During the show you react instantly. These need different screens: an editing space (Programs, Setup) and a performing space (Live, Music, Cues) with bigger targets and fewer options.

Nothing matters if selection is confusing

The fixture is the unit of thought: "those two beams on the right". A persistent fixture rail with clear selection state prevents the classic disaster of programming the wrong lights.

Safety needs to be physical

A Grand Master fader, a blackout button, undo/redo, and confirmation on destructive actions. On stage, a wrong tap in the dark must never be able to destroy the show.

08 · User flow

From blackout to first light.

The setup flow for a new user: the product must produce visible light fast, or trust dies at the door.

The fixture-first rule: nothing in Control renders until a fixture is selected, and once one is, the panels show only what that fixture can actually do. A cheap par never shows gobo controls. This one rule kills both empty screens and impossible actions.

09 · Wireframes

Finding the shell.

The structural question: how do you fit a lighting console's density on one screen without burying the panic buttons? Three structures.

Study 1: everything at once

Every panel on one screen, like a physical console. Authentic, and unusable on a laptop: everything too small to hit in the dark. Dropped.

Study 2: guided steps

A wizard: pick fixtures → pick property → adjust. Great for beginners, fatal for live use: three taps to reach a dimmer is two taps too many. Dropped.

Study 3: fixed shell + modes

Top bar (safety, always), sidebar (mode switch), fixture rail (selection, always), main area (the current mode). Density where you asked for it, panic buttons never move. Shipped.

10 · Design exploration

Two design languages, on purpose.

The most unusual visual decision in the product, and a calculated risk: the app deliberately contains two different design worlds.

Shipped

"Cyber console" (the app's own voice)

Dark background with a grid, pill buttons, uppercase monospace. Neon green #00ff88 as the lead color. Orbitron for headings and navigation, Share Tech Mono for data and sliders. It reads as an instrument, which is what it is.

Used everywhere: Control, Live, Music, Cues, Setup.
Shipped

"Daslight console" (the Programs tab)

The Programs tab mirrors the professional software Daslight 5: a warm palette of browns and yellow, banks, a scene grid, a fixture map, a curve editor, and a TOUCH mode. Completely different from the green world around it.

Used in one place, on purpose: where pros expect their own conventions.

Why break consistency on purpose? Because the Programs tab's users already have years of muscle memory in Daslight, and familiarity beats uniformity for them. It is a risk I track honestly: two visual languages in one app is a consistency cost, and it is covered below in Challenges.

13 · Real-time & touch

Tested with real lights, in a real dark room.

You cannot prototype a lighting console with static screens: the product is the feedback loop between a finger on glass and a beam moving in the room. So the prototype was always the real system, connected to real fixtures over a real USB-DMX interface, iterated gig after gig.

What only live testing revealed: the pan/tilt joystick needed a canvas with fine grid snapping, because finger precision in the dark is worse than anyone plans for. Sliders needed optimistic UI over the WebSocket, because even 100ms of wait between finger and light feels broken. And the tap-tempo button had to be huge, because you hit it with the side of your hand while doing something else.

Every control change is broadcast over WebSocket to all connected surfaces, so the phone remote, the tablet, and the desktop always show the same live state. Undo/redo works across all of them, which turned out to be the feature that makes people trust handing the phone remote to someone else.

14 · Challenges

The hard decisions, including the honest ones.

14.1Latency vs. truth: what does a slider show?

Considered: server-confirmed state
The slider shows the value the server confirmed. Always true, never fast. At 100-200ms round trip, dragging a dimmer felt like stirring honey. Unacceptable live.
Shipped: optimistic UI with reconciliation
The slider follows the finger instantly, the value streams over WebSocket, and the server state reconciles quietly. If the connection drops, a clear status pill flips to red and controls lock, so "fast" never becomes "lying".

14.2One design language or two?

Considered: one unified language everywhere
Cleaner, prouder, more "designed". But it would force Daslight users to relearn a workflow they already own, in the one tab where their muscle memory is the whole value.
Shipped: two languages, one boundary
The cyber-console language owns the app; the Daslight language owns exactly one tab. The boundary is the tab edge, never inside a screen. I keep an honest eye on the cost: a legacy Programs page still exists that the mobile app links to, and unifying those two is on the roadmap.

14.3How much product fits in the Music tab?

Considered: full DJ parity on every surface
The desktop Music mode is a 4-deck Serato-style view: waveforms, hot cues, crossfader, AI director. Shrinking that to a phone would produce an unusable postage stamp of a DJ booth.
Shipped: same intent, different depth per surface
Desktop gets the full DJ view. The phone gets three presets (AMBIENT / PARTY / RAVE) and a beat button. The tablet gets BPM + mic sync. It is a product-inside-a-product, and each surface gets the version its context can actually use.

14.4Hardware is part of the UX

Considered: browser-only, WebUSB
No install, beautiful story. But WebUSB support for DMX interfaces is patchy, and a gig cannot depend on a browser flag.
Shipped: a free Electron desktop app as the hardware bridge
The desktop app owns the USB-DMX connection and runs the local server; every other surface connects to it. Register, download, sign in with the same account, plug in. One extra install, zero flakiness at showtime.
15 · Results

A real console, running real shows.

Live and free during beta, with the full stack working: browser console, desktop hardware bridge, and phone and tablet remotes.

What shipped

  • The full desktop console: Control, Programs (Daslight-style), Live, Music, Cues, Setup, Settings
  • Phone remote and tablet layout, live over the same WebSocket protocol
  • Electron desktop app bridging real USB-DMX hardware
  • Fixture patching with per-fixture safety limits, groups, and a DMX monitor
  • 2D/3D rig visualizer (Three.js)
  • The 4-deck DJ Music mode with beat sync and an AI director
  • Cue stacks with GO/BACK, chase builder, tap tempo, scene banks
  • Personalization: 8 accent hues, density modes, dark/light, fixture color coding
  • Marketing site, auth, and account system shared across all surfaces

Lessons learned

  • Live products are designed at the worst moment. Every layout decision starts from "dark room, sweaty hands, right now". If it works there, it works everywhere.
  • Consistency is a tool, not a religion. Borrowing Daslight's language in one tab bought more usability than uniform branding would have. But two languages need a hard boundary, and honesty about the cost.
  • Real-time UX is a data problem wearing a design costume. Optimistic state, reconciliation, and connection truth-telling shaped the feel of the product more than any visual choice.

What's next

  • Unify the two Programs systems (the console tab and the legacy page)
  • Systematic i18n instead of mixed Hebrew/English strings
  • Show templates: save a whole gig and reuse it next weekend
